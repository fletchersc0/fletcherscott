About

Casual Sessional Tutor for Psychology | Computational Models of Cognition | Resource-Rational Sequential Learning | Human-Computer Interaction

I research how people process information. My current interest is in how processing specifically applies to political messages, especially those designed to mislead or provoke strong emotions. Currently, I am mapping how the order and repetition of messages, such as what shows up first or later, as well as how ideas cluster over time, changes the mental effort people devote to evaluating claims. By pinpointing these moments, information systems could critically place nudges or fact-checks exactly where they can trigger closer scrutiny.

The approach is to assess how computational models can be used to infer users’ real-time processing (e.g. how quickly they skim, when they slow down, and which cues grab their attention). Embedding a model of the learner into information platforms paves the way for adaptive systems that tailor corrections, explanations, or practice material to the moment a person is most receptive, turning generic facts into personalized learning aids.

Deployment of attention and memory on social media: an integrative review of latent-state learning for misinformation and affective polarization (2025)

Digital resilience relies on users' ability to resist credibility distortions when crisis narratives flood social feeds, heightening uncertainty and pushing people to lean on prior expectations as cognitive shortcuts. These expectations alter how people process information, shaping early perceptual encoding and higher-order veracity judgments to foster acceptance and defence of misinformation. While such heuristics are viewed as resource-efficient adaptations, their role in polarization and veracity perception remain poorly understood. We recast these biases within a latent-state framework in which the brain infers hidden causal structures from sequential input and re-uses them to minimize prediction error. Transferring predictions from familiar states to novel contexts streamlines inference but is likely to lead to polarized perspectives on news. Sequenced, consistently patterned feeds on social media provide the structured input that latent-cause mechanisms exploit, allowing users to infer hidden generative factors implicitly and carry those inferences into new contexts. Consequently, users give priority to messages marked by ingroup signals or high perceptual fluency in a way that deepens partisan divides. This paper advances an empirical protocol for studying biased information use in dynamic, digitally mediated environments that (i) tracks latent-state formation with sequential Bayesian modelling, (ii) tests whether social alignment and emotional valence serve as hidden dimensions that gate information uptake, and (iii) identifies candidate neural substrates. Situating polarization within normative latent-state inference yields testable approaches for developing interventions that may strengthen resilience to political polarization while preserving cognitive efficiency.
